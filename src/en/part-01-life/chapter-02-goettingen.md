# Chapter 2: Göttingen Years

> Difficulty: ★ | Paths: 🟢🟡🔴 | Prerequisites: Chapter 1

## Between Theology and Mathematics

In 1846, Riemann arrived at the University of Göttingen. He was registered as a student of theology — this was what his father expected, and the pastorate was the family tradition. But almost immediately, he began attending lectures in mathematics.

Göttingen's mathematical faculty shone brightly. Carl Friedrich Gauss, though nearly seventy, remained the towering figure of German science. He held the chair of astronomy and directed the Göttingen Observatory, but his lectures still drew serious students. Moritz Stern — the first Jewish full professor at Göttingen — also taught mathematics there.

Riemann attended courses in both faculties. The balance could not hold. In a letter to his father, he confessed that he could not resist the pull of mathematics. After a long inner struggle, Riemann asked permission to change his course of study. His father, who loved his son and respected his judgment, consented.

## Berlin and the Masters

In 1847, after one year at Göttingen, Riemann transferred to the University of Berlin. Berlin was then the undisputed center of German mathematics.

Three giants held the mathematical stage. Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet was one of the founders of analytic number theory — the first to systematically apply the methods of analysis (infinite series, integrals, limits) to problems about whole numbers. Carl Jacobi had made epochal contributions to elliptic functions and differential equations. Jakob Steiner was the greatest geometer of his age.

And there was also Ferdinand Eisenstein — a young genius whose work in number theory and elliptic functions had prompted Gauss to name him one of "only three first-rate mathematicians" (the others being Archimedes and Newton).

Riemann spent four semesters in Berlin, from 1847 to 1849. He attended Dirichlet's lectures on number theory and analysis, Jacobi's on analytical mechanics, and Eisenstein's on elliptic functions. Years later, he would say that Dirichlet's influence had been the deepest.

## Dirichlet's Influence

Dirichlet's teaching style was the polar opposite of Gauss's. Gauss was famously obscure — his proofs arrived like lightning bolts, with the intermediate reasoning concealed from the audience. Dirichlet, by contrast, was renowned for clarity, elegance, and systematic exposition. His lectures were works of art.

What Dirichlet gave Riemann was a vision: the vision of analysis applied to number theory. From Dirichlet, Riemann learned how to use infinite series, integral representations, and the machinery of complex functions to study the integers. This idea — that the continuous could illuminate the discrete — would reach its apotheosis in Riemann's 1859 paper.

Dirichlet's own work on Fourier series convergence and his introduction of the Dirichlet principle (a method for solving boundary-value problems) would directly shape Riemann's doctoral dissertation.

## The Doctoral Thesis: Foundations of Complex Analysis

Riemann returned to Göttingen in 1849. Gauss was now giving few lectures, but he agreed to supervise Riemann's doctoral work. The dissertation, completed in 1851, was titled *Grundlagen für eine allgemeine Theorie der Functionen einer veränderlichen complexen Grösse* (Foundations for a General Theory of Functions of One Complex Variable).

The thesis became a landmark. In it, Riemann:

- Gave a rigorous definition of holomorphic functions
- Introduced a geometric approach to function theory, interpreting complex functions as conformal maps between domains in the plane
- Proved the Riemann Mapping Theorem: every simply connected domain (except the whole plane) can be mapped conformally onto the unit disk
- Generalized Dirichlet's principle to solve boundary-value problems — a method later vindicated by Hilbert

Reading the thesis, Gauss — who had supervised dozens of doctoral students over his long career — wrote that it demonstrated "an original, profound, truly mathematical mind." From Gauss, such praise was almost unheard of.

In December 1851, Riemann successfully defended his dissertation and received the degree of Doctor of Philosophy.

## From Student to Scholar

Now Riemann had to earn the right to teach. He remained at Göttingen as an unsalaried lecturer — a *Privatdozent* — and began work on his Habilitation.

The Habilitation thesis turned to trigonometric series (what we now call Fourier series) and the question of which functions they could represent. This work would give the world the rigorous definition of the Riemann integral.

But it was his trial lecture — the *Probevorlesung* required for the teaching license — that proved even more momentous than the thesis.

---

> **Key points**: Riemann moved from theology to mathematics, studied in Berlin under Dirichlet (whose clarity and analytic-number-theoretic vision deeply shaped him), returned to Göttingen, and under Gauss's supervision wrote a doctoral thesis that laid the foundations of geometric complex analysis. Gauss praised it as the work of "a truly mathematical mind."
