# Chapter 1: The Young Riemann

> Difficulty: ★ | Paths: 🟢🟡🔴 | Prerequisites: None

## Birth and Family

Georg Friedrich Bernhard Riemann was born on September 17, 1826, in the village of Breselenz, in the Kingdom of Hanover (now part of Lower Saxony, Germany). It was a hamlet of a few hundred souls, set among the flat farmland and forests east of the Elbe River.

His father, Friedrich Bernhard Riemann, was a Lutheran pastor who had fought in the Napoleonic Wars before studying theology. His mother, Charlotte Ebell, was the daughter of a court counselor and had received a solid education at home. Bernhard was the second of six children.

The Riemann household was poor in money but rich in books and ideas. The elder Riemann taught his children Latin, history, and mathematics himself. The disciplined habits of thought that Bernhard absorbed from his father would remain with him for life. As a child, he was frail, withdrawn, and sensitive — more at home among books than in the rough games of village boys. Throughout his life he retained this quiet, introspective temperament.

## Early Education and Talent

When Riemann was six, the family moved to Quickborn, where his father had been assigned a new parish. Here Bernhard entered the village school. His mathematical gifts quickly outstripped anything the local schoolmaster could teach.

Around the age of ten, a tutor named Schulz was engaged to instruct him in arithmetic. According to a well-known story, Schulz soon discovered that the thin, quiet boy could already solve every problem he was given — often by routes simpler and more elegant than the ones in the textbook. On one occasion, Riemann solved a supposedly difficult problem within minutes of the tutor's departure, and by the next morning had generalized the result. This drive toward generality — the habit of never settling for a single instance — became the hallmark of his mathematical thinking.

These were years of almost entirely solitary exploration. There were no advanced books in the village, no mathematical companions. He learned from a handful of textbooks and from his own reflection. Perhaps it was precisely this isolation that fostered his unique intuition — a capacity to approach problems from first principles, unencumbered by received wisdom.

## Lüneburg and Legendre's *Théorie des Nombres*

In 1840, at fourteen, Riemann left home to live with his grandmother in Hannover and attend the Lyceum. Two years later, after his grandmother's death, he transferred to the Johanneum Gymnasium in Lüneburg — and this became the decisive turn of his youth.

The Johanneum possessed an unusually good library. The director of the school, E. Schmalfuß, noticed the quiet student's unusual talent. He not only gave Riemann free access to the library's mathematical collection but lent him books from his own shelves. The most important of these was Adrien-Marie Legendre's *Théorie des Nombres* (third edition, 1830) — a comprehensive treatise of more than eight hundred pages, written in French.

Riemann returned the book after six days. When Schmalfuß asked whether he had understood it, Riemann answered that he had. Two years later, as he was leaving Lüneburg, he told the director: "It is a wonderful book. I have mastered it completely."

Legendre's treatise contained, among much else, the conjectured form of the Prime Number Theorem:

$$
\pi(x) \sim \frac{x}{\log x - A}
$$

where $A$ is a constant, as well as discussions of primes in arithmetic progressions. These deep questions about the prime numbers planted a seed in the young Riemann's mind. More than twenty years later, that seed would flower into his epochal paper of 1859.

## Early Signs of a Mathematical Mind

From the few notebooks that survive from this period (preserved in part at the Göttingen University Library), we can discern several traits of Riemann's mathematical temperament:

- **The drive toward generality**: He was never content to solve a single problem. He habitually sought the law behind the example and tested each conclusion by multiple independent methods.
- **An instinct for rigor**: Even at an age when rote memorization of formulas was the educational norm, Riemann demanded to know *why*. When classmates were satisfied remembering a formula, he would be found working through its derivation on a scrap of paper.
- **Prodigious mental calculation**: Multiple witnesses attest to his ability to compute complicated numerical expressions entirely in his head. This capacity would later prove crucial in his famous hand-calculation of the first several zeros of the zeta function.

## Toward Göttingen

In the spring of 1846, the nineteen-year-old Riemann graduated from the Johanneum. His classical languages were excellent; his mathematics had long since passed the secondary level. Following his father's wishes, he was to study theology at the University of Göttingen and eventually succeed his father in the pastorate.

But Riemann's heart had already made its choice. In the library at Lüneburg and in Director Schmalfuß's study, he had glimpsed the vast world of mathematics — primes, infinite series, the mysteries of functions — and he found it more compelling than any theological doctrine.

After Easter 1846, Riemann matriculated at the Georg-August-Universität Göttingen. On paper he was a theology student. But he also enrolled in mathematics lectures. Göttingen was then one of the leading mathematical centers in the German-speaking world. Carl Friedrich Gauss — "the Prince of Mathematicians," now nearly seventy — was still lecturing there. Riemann had arrived at the threshold of the mathematical temple.

But the road from theology to mathematics, from village pastor's son to figure who would change the course of mathematical history — that road still lay ahead.
